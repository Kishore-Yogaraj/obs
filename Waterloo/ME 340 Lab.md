**Limitations**
- Dimensional accuracy
- Surface finish quality

**Improvements over time**
- Well and gate system
- Vent
- Risers

**Casting parameters**
- Pouring rates
- Superheat (difference between the pouring temperature and its liquidus temperature)
	- Liquidus temperature is the lowest temperature at which a metal or alloy is completely liquid during heating 
	- Below this temperature, the first solid crystals begin to form durng cooling
	- Pouring temperature must be above liquidus temperature to ensure the metal flows properly and fills the mold
	- Knowing the liquidus temperature helps in
		- Choosing the proper pouring temperatures
		- Controlling cooling rates
		- Preventing casting defects like incomplete filling or poor grain structure

**Parameters of focus**
- Pouring temperature (140 degrees C or 190 degrees C)
	- Expected melting temperature of pewter stock is just below 140 degrees C
	- Theoretical melting temperature is 139 degrees C
- Mould temperature (65 degrees C)


### Casting Operations

**Preparing silicone moulds**
Moulds need to be pre-heated by the TAs

Note down the temperature of the mould to ensure it is suitable before removing from the furnace

Apply Talc powder on the inner surface of the mould cavity (this increase wettability as well as act as a but of release agent )
- Wettability is the measure of how well a liquid spreads and adheres to a sold surface 
- How well molten can make contact with a mold or another solid surface without forming gaps or pulling away 

Turn the mould faced down and tap it against the prepared tray to remove any excess talc powder
- Make sure mould does not have moisture or water droplets prior to pouring of molten metal

Place the mould halves together to form the mould cavity

If given cylindrical mould, then use the ABS tube which will act as a flask. Some talc powder on the outside of the mould might reduce friction

For rectangular mould use the provided bar clamps 

**Preparing the Pewter for Casting**
Place appropriate amount of pewter into the stainless steel pitcher (frothing pitcher) and place the pitcher on the hot plate to melt the pewter.
- Use the IR thermometer to measure the temperature of the pewter
- Note that the surface temperature as measured by the IR thermometer is normally 80-100 degrees C lower than the metal's average temperature
- To get an accurate reading we can stir the molten metal 
- Or we can use a thermocouple which we can submerge into the molten metal

Use the scoopula (skimmer) to skim the surface to remove as much of the slag (impurities and oxides on the surface as possible then stir the molten pewter slowly to reach an even temperature

After reaching the specified temperature, pour the molten pewter into the prepared mold slowly and continuously and let the part solidify

Depending on the mould pre-heat temperature, a complete solidification can take up to 45 minutes. Consults with the lab instructor or TA before demolding parts.

This experiment demonstrates that casting quality is sensitive to process parameters such as pouring temperatures, mould temperatures, sprue diameter and sprue orientation. From the 12 casting conditions tested, volumetric shrinkage ranged from -9.6% to +14.8% which demonstrates how the different casting conditions can influence the casting results.

            A key conclusion is that the higher pouring temperatures at ~190°C and preheated moulds at 65°C led to reduced shrinkage which was as low as –1.5% and improved dimensional accuracy especially in more complex geometries like Geometry C. An example of this is in Condition 8, which had a high pouring temperature and a preheated mould and achieved the lowest shrinkage for Geometry C. Condition 1 used a low pouring temperature and a room temperature mould which resulted in the highest shrinkage at 23.1%.

            Sprue diameter also had a noticeable impact on shrinkage consistency. A smaller sprue diameter of 3.2mm produced a more table shrinkage range between –1.5% and +14.7% while the 6.4 mm sprue diameter introduced greater variability as this results in both the highest and lowest shrinkage values. Although a smaller sprue is more restrictive, the experiment suggest that it regulates flow and minimizes turbulent entry which can cause porosity and dimensional errors. The orientation of the sprue also had noticeable contributions to the casting quality. All horizontally casted parts which were condition 11 and 12 showed expansion which was likely due to buoyancy effects and improper mould filling without gravity assistance. Vertically oriented sprues had more consistent expected shrinkage that align with typical solidification patterns.

            Cold shuts, inclusions, sink marks, and dimensional inaccuracies were noted as a result of insufficient thermal energy, inadequate mould clamping, poor gating design, and inconsistent pouring techniques. For example, cold shuts were prevalent in low-temperature pours with narrow sprues like in condition 1 and 6 and inclusions often occurred when ventilation or slag removal was inadequate.

            In summary, from this experiment it can be determined that the optimal casting conditions for pewter are as follows:

·       Pouring temperatures of 165-170°C

·       Preheated mould at 65°C

·       3.2 mm sprue diameter with a slight taper for stable flow

·       Vertical orientation to assist in gravity-driven filling and to reduce porosity

These parameters led to improved surface finish, minimized shrinkage, and greater dimensional accuracy. These results demonstrate the importance of precise control over vesting conditions and prove how small deviations can lead to significant defects in the final cast
