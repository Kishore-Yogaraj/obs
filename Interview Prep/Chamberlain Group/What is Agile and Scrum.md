What is agile and scrum?
**Agile** is a project management and software development methodology focused on iterative development, flexibility, and collaboration. It breaks projects into smaller, manageable increments called **sprints** or **iterations**, which are typically 1-4 weeks long. At the end of each sprint, a working piece of the product is delivered, and the team evaluates the progress, gathers feedback, and adjusts plans as necessary. Agile emphasizes constant communication between teams, stakeholders, and customers to ensure the product evolves based on real-world feedback.

Within Agile, one of the most popular frameworks is **Scrum**. Scrum provides a structured way of managing work through defined roles, events, and artifacts:

- The **Scrum Team** typically includes a **Product Owner** (who represents the stakeholders and prioritizes the work), a **Scrum Master** (who ensures the team follows Scrum practices and removes obstacles), and the **Development Team** (who works on delivering the product increment).
    
- Scrum uses **sprints**, which are short, time-boxed periods (usually 1-2 weeks) during which a set amount of work is completed. Each sprint starts with a **Sprint Planning** meeting, where the team decides what tasks will be completed during that sprint, based on the highest priority items from the product backlog.
    
- Every day, the team holds a brief **Daily Standup (or Daily Scrum)** meeting to discuss progress, challenges, and goals for the day. At the end of the sprint, there’s a **Sprint Review**, where the team demonstrates the work completed, and a **Sprint Retrospective**, where they reflect on what went well and what could be improved for the next sprint.
    

In my experience, Agile and Scrum have been highly effective in managing complex projects. For example, in Watonomous, we used Agile methodologies to develop autonomous vehicle perception systems, where requirements and priorities could change quickly based on test results. Scrum provided a framework to break down tasks into sprints, allowing us to iteratively deliver and refine features while keeping communication clear across the team.
